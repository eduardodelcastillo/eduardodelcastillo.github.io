Quick Questions

How much does Fifth Capital charge for its service?

Fifth Capital charges a management fee of 0.85% to 1.50%, depending on your balance, and is prorated across the entire year and is charged at the end of each calendar quarter (every three months). In other words, every three months Fifth Capital charges 0.2125% to 0.375% based on your average balance for the period. If you withdraw all of your money before the end of the quarter you are charged a prorated fee only for the days your money was managed by Fifth Capital.

Fifth Capital’s single management fee known as a “Wrap Fee” program covers everything you need including commissions, trades, transfers, rebalancing, advice, account administration, etc. Unlike other companies Fifth Capital does not charge you transaction fees to buy and sell securities.

*The annual fee normally comes out of the return. For example, if your portfolio gained 10%, the net gain would be 8.50% based on a 1.50% annual fee.

What are my fees based on?

Your fees are based on your average balance of your entire Fifth Capital account, not the gain or loss.

Am I still charged the whole fee even if I don't use the service for the whole year?

No. Fifth Capital’s fee is a straightforward 0.85% to 1.50%, depending on your balance, and is prorated across the year based on the average daily balance in your account, and a portion of this is charged quarterly (every 3 months). So if you withdraw money from your account, your average daily balance will decrease and your fee for the quarter will reduce accordingly. If you withdraw your entire balance before the end of the quarter we will simply charge the prorated fee at that time.

What kind of trading commissions will I incur with at Fifth Capital?

You will pay no commissions on trades made on your behalf by Fifth Capital. < “WRAP FEE”> - Fifth Capital’s single management fee known as a “Wrap Fee” program covers everything you need including commissions, trades, transfers, rebalancing, advice, account administration, etc. Unlike other companies Fifth Capital does not charge you transaction fees to buy and sell securities.

Are there custodial fees associated with my accounts?

You will pay no custodial fees on accounts held with Interactive Brokers.

Are there exit fees due if I chose to close my Fifth Capital account?

No, there are no exit fees associated with closing accounts. If you withdraw your entire balance before the end of the quarter we will simply charge the prorated fee at that time.

How are fees the calculated?

Every three months you are charged an advisory fee equal to 0.2125% to 0.375% of the annual rate multiplied by the net market value of your average balance for the period.

For example: If your average daily balance for the quarter ending June 30th is $55,000, the quarterly fee is calculated as: $55,000 multiplied by 0.375% = $20.63, less any credits received through our “WRAP” program.

When does Fifth Capital charge its advisory fees?

Advisory fees for a particular quarter are charged on the first business day of the following month. For example, the advisory fee for investing during the April – June quarter of 2014 was charged on July 1, 2014 (the first business day of July). Our Account Services department will send an invoice with the calculation of the fees debited from the account. The invoice is only for your records as all fees are automatically debited from the account.

We provide active management of all client accounts. We provide unique features that simplify your life as an investor and help you get better returns. All of our research is done in-house; our risks management tools are executed simultaneously across all accounts; Our core-strategies make it simple to buy/sell or manage risk across accounts. That’s impossible to do when advisors run multiple strategies for many different clients – so you get focused allocation and risk management.

We are performance oriented, meaning that we aim to add in client accounts; Our performance figures are actual and not proforma or backtested “could’ve achieved” type figures as many online advisors put out. How does Fifth Capital Manage risk?

One of the first questions we ask ourselves before making an investment is, “What are the odds that this investment could fail because of catastrophe risk?” In other words, what are the major things that could go wrong?

We feel its crucial that our clients understand our views on risk, how we define it and how we manage it. Risk has no universal definition, but we define risk as a “permanent loss of principal.”

As value managers, we focus on risk as well as return. To the extent that most investors think about risk at all, they seem confused about it. Some insist that risk and return are always positively correlated; the greater the risk, the greater the return. This is, in fact, a basic tenet of Modern Portfolio Theory taught in nearly all business schools, yet it is not always true. Others mistakenly equate risk with volatility, emphasizing the ‘risk’ of security price fluctuations while ignoring the risk of making overpriced, ill-conceived, or poorly managed investments.

A positive correlation between risk and return would hold consistently only in an efficient market. Since the financial markets are inefficient a good deal of the time, investors cannot simply select a level of risk and be confident that it will be reflected in the accompanying returns. Risk and return must instead be assessed independently for every investment.

In point of fact, greater risk does not guarantee greater return. To the contrary, risk erodes return by causing losses. By itself risk does not create incremental return; only price can accomplish that.

"Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."
- Warren Buffet

Risk is a perception in each investor’s mind that results from analysis of the probability and amount of potential loss from an investment. If an exploratory oil well proves to be a dry hole, it is called risky. If a bond defaults or a stock plunges in price, they are called risky. But if the well is a gusher, the bond matures on schedule, and the stock rallies strongly, can we say they weren’t risky when the investment was made? Not at all. The point is, in most cases no more is known about the risk of an investment after it is concluded than was known when it was made.

There are only a few things we can do to counteract risk: diversify adequately, hedge when appropriate, and invest with a margin of safety. It is precisely because we do not and cannot know all the risks of an investment that we strive to invest at a discount. The bargain element helps provide a cushion for when things go wrong.

The biggest mistake’s an investor makes in the market are (a) overpaying to much, (b) not buying with a margin of safety, and (c) being wrong and not doing anything about it.

To illustrate how the margin of safety works, let’s take an example of an investment our company made in Western Digitial, “WDC”. At the time of our analysis, we determined that “WDC” had an intrinsic value of $105.00 per share. The stock was trading at $62.89 when we made the purchase. The purchase price offered us a discount of 40% from its intrinsic value. In a nutshell, the margin of safety is a percentage discount from the company intrinsic value. This is the reason why the price paid affects the margin of safety and risk/return profile. Overpaying for a company gives little room for error and is not considered intelligent investing.
